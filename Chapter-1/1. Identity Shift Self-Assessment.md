## 1. Identity Shift Self-Assessment  
*Before you dive into the drills, take a quick reading on where you are right now.*

For each statement, rate how true it feels today, using a scale of 1 (not at all) to 5 (completely). No judgment—just data.

| Statement | 1–5 |
|-----------|-----|
| When someone asks what I do, I naturally describe it in terms of the hours I sell or my billing rate. |  |
| A good month, in my gut, means high utilization and billable hours, not necessarily client outcomes. |  |
| I feel a pang of guilt or anxiety when a deliverable took less time than budgeted, even if the result was excellent. |  |
| The phrase “I’m overpriced” crosses my mind when I think about how quickly AI could do parts of my work. |  |
| I measure my career achievements mostly by projects staffed and hours logged, rather than by specific business risks averted. |  |
| The idea of quoting a fee without referencing time feels uncomfortable—like I’m hiding something. |  |
| When clients question an invoice, my first instinct is to mentally defend the hours, not the impact. |  |

*Now, add up your total.*  
**7–15:** Your identity is already shifting—this chapter will cement that.  
**16–25:** The billable-hour anchor still holds weight, but you’re aware of it. The drills will release it.  
**26–35:** The anchor is deep. That’s normal after decades of conditioning. Give yourself extra patience and repeat the exercises more than once.

Take this self-assessment again after completing all four drills. Notice how the numbers (and your gut reactions) change.
