## 1. Billable-Hour Identity Diagnostic

Before you rewrite your story, you need to see how deeply the billable hour has written it for you. Read each statement and check the box that best reflects your gut reaction. There are no right answers—only honest ones.

| When I think about my professional value… | Rarely | Sometimes | Often | Always |
|--------------------------------------------|--------|-----------|-------|--------|
| I equate a “good day” with a high number of billable hours. | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |
| I feel anxious or guilty when I solve a client problem quickly because there are fewer hours to bill. | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |
| I describe my proudest achievements by mentioning the time, team size, or effort involved first. | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |
| When a client questions a fee, my immediate instinct is to defend the hours worked. | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |
| I secretly believe that charging for outcomes rather than time is unfair to the client—or to me. | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |
| I measure my career progress by utilization rate and realized rate more than by client impact. | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |
| The thought of AI reducing task time makes me feel professionally diminished. | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |
| I have avoided proposing a fixed or value-based fee because I couldn’t imagine how to justify it without a timesheet. | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ | ☐ |

If you ticked “Often” or “Always” on more than three of these, you’ve been carrying the billable-hour anchor. That’s not a failure; it’s an honest starting point. This workbook exists to cut the chain.
