## 3. The Five-Factor Scoring Guide

For each service line, you’ll score all five factors on a 1–5 scale. Use the definitions and prompts below. Be ruthless. If you’re torn between two scores, choose the one that reflects the client’s likely perception, not your internal narrative.

### Factor 1: Standardization
*How much of this service follows a repeatable, codified process?*

- **1** – Entirely bespoke every time; no template or checklist could capture the work.
- **2** – Mostly bespoke, but some process steps are repeatable.
- **3** – A mix: significant custom work layered on a standardized base.
- **4** – Largely templated; a smart junior with a playbook could execute most of it.
- **5** – Fully standardized; the work follows a clear decision tree or checklist end-to-end.

**Prompt questions:** If I wrote a detailed manual, could someone less experienced produce a comparable output? Is this work frequently offshored or given to the most junior team members? Does the client expect a “process” or a “verdict”?

### Factor 2: Data Dependency
*How much of the insight comes from publicly available or structured data that AI could ingest?*

- **1** – All key insight comes from proprietary, privileged, or non-public information.
- **2** – Mostly proprietary data, but some public sources are used.
- **3** – Roughly equal reliance on public and proprietary sources.
- **4** – Mostly public/structured data, with some proprietary overlay.
- **5** – All relevant data is publicly available and structured; no privileged access required.

**Prompt questions:** Could a competitor with the same public databases produce a similar output? Is the value in the data itself, or in what I do with it? Would the client lose much if they ran the same search through an AI tool?

### Factor 3: Judgment Requirement
*How much does the service demand contextual calibration, risk-weighting, and the human reassurance that no machine can provide?*

- **1** – Purely process-driven; no judgment needed beyond following the rules.
- **2** – Minor judgment calls, mostly about sequencing or format.
- **3** – Moderate judgment; some interpretation of ambiguous guidance, but within established norms.
- **4** – High judgment; requires weighing non-obvious risks, political dynamics, or personality factors.
- **5** – Entirely judgment-dependent; the outcome hinges on irreplaceable human instinct, novel analysis, or the weight of personal accountability.

**Prompt questions:** If an AI produced the analytical output, would the client still need me to say “this is the right path”? Does this service involve predicting how a specific regulator, judge, or board member will react based on unwritten history? Would a mistake here expose me personally to reputational or liability consequences that shape my decision-making?

### Factor 4: Risk Transfer
*How much liability does your firm absorb that the client would otherwise carry?*

- **1** – Client bears all risk; we provide information but no reliance signature.
- **2** – Minimal risk transfer; our work is advisory and non-binding.
- **3** – Moderate risk; errors could cause client harm, but primary liability remains with them.
- **4** – Significant risk; we certify, opine, or deliver work product that the client relies on for major decisions.
- **5** – Full risk transfer; we are the named party in an audit, certification, or regulatory filing, and our balance sheet backs the work.

**Prompt questions:** If this deliverable is wrong, who gets sued? Who loses their license or equity? Does the client pay for the document, or for the insurance policy my signature represents?

### Factor 5: Client Perception
*How does the client currently view this service—as a commodity or a premium capability?*

- **1** – Client sees this as a commodity; they’d push back hard on any non-hourly fee.
- **2** – Client is skeptical but open to discussion if the value is clearly articulated.
- **3** – Client is neutral; they haven’t expressed a strong preference either way.
- **4** – Client values this service and would consider fixed or value-based pricing.
- **5** – Client already requests fixed-fee or value-based arrangements for this service.

**Prompt questions:** If I proposed an outcome-based fee tomorrow, would the client’s first reaction be confusion, curiosity, or relief? Have they ever questioned the hours on this type of work? Do they compare our fees to other firms on an hourly basis, or do they evaluate the result?
