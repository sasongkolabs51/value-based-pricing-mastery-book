**Objection 2: “Will this hurt my draw? I rely on billable hours to fund my income.”**

*Response:* “I understand that fear—it’s the reason I’m proposing an opt‑in pilot with a compensation hold‑harmless. Your base draw remains unchanged. The variable component is upside only, tied to metrics like outcome value originated and realization rate. In my own pilot, the outcome‑based engagement delivered a [X]% higher margin than the old model would have, on fewer hours. My effective income per hour of my own time went up, not down. We can design the metrics so that succeeding in this model improves your economics, not harms them.”

**Objection 3: “This might work for regulatory work, but never for litigation.”**

*Response:* “That’s a fair question, and I’m not asking anyone to force a square peg into a round hole. The framework is designed to be applied where the audit shows high judgment intensity and high risk transfer—those exist in litigation, particularly in high‑stakes disputes. I’d be happy to walk through a litigation matter with you using the same diagnostic. If the audit scores suggest it’s a fit, we can design a small pilot together. If not, we’ll know it’s not the right model for that practice. I’m not asking for a blanket conversion; I’m asking for a willingness to diagnose.”

**Objection 4: “What about our valuation multiples? Won’t unpredictable revenue hurt us?”**

*Response:* “Ironically, the opposite is true. Hourly billing revenue is becoming more unpredictable because realization rates are declining and write‑offs are increasing. Outcome‑based fees, especially when structured as fixed fees or recurring retainers, actually provide more predictable revenue. A portfolio of fixed‑fee outcome engagements with high realization gives a clearer, more reliable picture of forward revenue than a book of hourly matters subject to growing discount pressure. Our valuation multiple is more at risk from declining realization than from a shift to outcome pricing.”

**Objection 5: “Our entire compensation system would need to be rebuilt. That’s a massive undertaking.”**

*Response:* “That’s why I’m not proposing an overnight overhaul. The pilot is a contained test. We run it for 12 months with a small cohort using a simple overlay on the existing system. At the end of the pilot, we have data on what works, what doesn’t, and what a broader compensation redesign should look like. We don’t rebuild the engine while the car is moving; we test a prototype in a controlled environment first. The Charter gives us that controlled environment.”
